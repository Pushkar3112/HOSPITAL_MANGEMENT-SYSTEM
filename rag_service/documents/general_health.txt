SECTION 1: BLOOD PRESSURE
==============================================================

WHAT IS BLOOD PRESSURE?
Blood pressure is the force of blood pushing against the walls of arteries as the heart
pumps blood. It is measured using two numbers:
- Systolic pressure (upper number): Pressure when the heart beats
- Diastolic pressure (lower number): Pressure when the heart rests between beats
Measured in millimeters of mercury (mmHg), e.g., 120/80 mmHg.

BLOOD PRESSURE CATEGORIES:
| Category              | Systolic (mmHg)    | Diastolic (mmHg) |
|-----------------------|--------------------|------------------|
| Normal                | Less than 120      | Less than 80     |
| Elevated              | 120–129            | Less than 80     |
| High (Stage 1 HTN)    | 130–139            | 80–89            |
| High (Stage 2 HTN)    | 140 or higher      | 90 or higher     |
| Hypertensive Crisis   | Above 180          | Above 120        |
| Low (Hypotension)     | Below 90           | Below 60         |

NORMAL BLOOD PRESSURE: Less than 120/80 mmHg
HIGH BLOOD PRESSURE: Above 140/90 mmHg (requires treatment)
LOW BLOOD PRESSURE: Below 90/60 mmHg

SYMPTOMS OF HIGH BLOOD PRESSURE (HYPERTENSION):
- Often called the "silent killer" — most people have NO symptoms
- When symptoms occur: Severe headache, nosebleeds, visual changes, chest pain, shortness of breath
- Hypertensive emergency: BP above 180/120 with organ damage symptoms — SEEK EMERGENCY CARE

SYMPTOMS OF LOW BLOOD PRESSURE (HYPOTENSION):
- Dizziness or lightheadedness (especially on standing up — orthostatic hypotension)
- Fainting or near-fainting
- Blurred vision
- Nausea
- Fatigue
- Cold, clammy, pale skin
- Rapid or shallow breathing

RISK FACTORS FOR HIGH BLOOD PRESSURE:
- Age (risk increases with age)
- Obesity
- Sedentary lifestyle
- High salt (sodium) diet
- Excessive alcohol
- Smoking
- Family history of hypertension
- Stress
- Diabetes or kidney disease
- Sleep apnea

MANAGING BLOOD PRESSURE:
- Reduce sodium intake (less than 2,300 mg per day; ideally 1,500 mg)
- DASH diet (Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension): Rich in fruits, vegetables, whole grains, low-fat dairy
- Exercise regularly (30 minutes of moderate exercise most days)
- Maintain healthy weight
- Limit alcohol (men: max 2 drinks/day; women: 1 drink/day)
- Quit smoking
- Manage stress
- Monitor blood pressure at home with a validated device
- Take prescribed medications regularly — do not stop without doctor's advice

MEDICATIONS FOR HYPERTENSION (commonly used):
- ACE inhibitors (Ramipril, Enalapril)
- ARBs (Losartan, Telmisartan)
- Calcium channel blockers (Amlodipine)
- Beta-blockers (Atenolol, Metoprolol)
- Diuretics (Hydrochlorothiazide, Furosemide)

SECTION 2: CHOLESTEROL
==============================================================

WHAT IS CHOLESTEROL?
Cholesterol is a waxy, fat-like substance found in your blood. Your body needs some cholesterol
to build healthy cells, but too much cholesterol increases your risk of heart disease.
Cholesterol is carried through the bloodstream by proteins called lipoproteins.

TYPES OF CHOLESTEROL:
- LDL (Low-Density Lipoprotein): "Bad cholesterol" — builds up in artery walls (plaque)
- HDL (High-Density Lipoprotein): "Good cholesterol" — helps remove LDL from arteries
- Triglycerides: Another type of fat in blood — high levels increase heart disease risk
- Total cholesterol: Sum of all cholesterol types

CHOLESTEROL NORMAL RANGES:
| Measure          | Optimal             | Borderline High    | High               |
|------------------|---------------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| Total Cholesterol| Less than 200 mg/dL | 200–239 mg/dL      | 240 mg/dL or higher|
| LDL Cholesterol  | Less than 100 mg/dL | 130–159 mg/dL      | 160 mg/dL or higher|
| HDL Cholesterol  | Above 60 mg/dL (ideal); Below 40 mg/dL = low (risky)  |
| Triglycerides    | Less than 150 mg/dL | 150–199 mg/dL      | 200 mg/dL or higher|

- NORMAL TOTAL CHOLESTEROL: Less than 200 mg/dL
- NORMAL LDL: Less than 100 mg/dL (below 70 if heart disease is present)
- GOOD HDL: Above 60 mg/dL

SYMPTOMS OF HIGH CHOLESTEROL:
- High cholesterol itself has NO symptoms
- Detected only by blood test (lipid profile)
- First sign may be a heart attack or stroke

FOODS TO REDUCE CHOLESTEROL:
- Increase: Oats, barley, nuts (walnuts, almonds), olive oil, fatty fish (salmon, mackerel),
  flaxseeds, beans/lentils, avocado, fruits, vegetables
- Reduce: Red meat, full-fat dairy, butter, ghee in excess, fried foods, processed foods,
  trans fats (partially hydrogenated oils — found in commercial baked goods, margarine)

MEDICATIONS FOR HIGH CHOLESTEROL:
- Statins (Atorvastatin, Rosuvastatin, Simvastatin) — most common
- Ezetimibe — reduces intestinal cholesterol absorption
- PCSK9 inhibitors (Evolocumab, Alirocumab) — for very high LDL

SECTION 3: BODY MASS INDEX (BMI)
==============================================================

WHAT IS BMI?
Body Mass Index (BMI) is a measure of body fat based on height and weight.
Formula: BMI = Weight (kg) / Height (m)²

INTERNATIONAL BMI CATEGORIES:
| Category      | BMI Range        |
|---------------|------------------|
| Underweight   | Below 18.5       |
| Normal Weight | 18.5 to 24.9     |
| Overweight    | 25.0 to 29.9     |
| Obese Class 1 | 30.0 to 34.9     |
| Obese Class 2 | 35.0 to 39.9     |
| Obese Class 3 | 40.0 and above   |

ASIAN INDIAN BMI CATEGORIES (Lower thresholds apply for Indians):
- Normal weight: 18.5 to 22.9
- Overweight: 23.0 to 24.9
- Obese: 25.0 and above

(Indians carry more abdominal fat at lower BMI than Western populations — greater health risk)

WAIST CIRCUMFERENCE (Better indicator of abdominal obesity):
- High risk for South Asians:
  Men: Above 90 cm (35.5 inches)
  Women: Above 80 cm (31.5 inches)

LIMITATIONS OF BMI:
- Does not distinguish between muscle and fat mass
- Athletes may have high BMI but low fat percentage
- Older adults may have normal BMI but high fat percentage
- Best used alongside waist circumference and body fat percentage

SECTION 4: COMMON COLD VS FLU (INFLUENZA)
==============================================================

COMPARISON OF COLD AND FLU:
| Feature          | Common Cold                    | Flu (Influenza)               |
|------------------|--------------------------------|-------------------------------|
| Onset            | Gradual (1-3 days)             | Sudden (within hours)         |
| Fever            | Rare or mild                   | High (38-40°C), sudden        |
| Headache         | Uncommon                       | Prominent                     |
| Fatigue          | Mild                           | Severe, can be incapacitating |
| Runny Nose       | Very common, watery to thick   | Sometimes                     |
| Sneezing         | Very common                    | Common                        |
| Sore Throat      | Common                         | Sometimes                     |
| Cough            | Mild to moderate, hacking      | Common, can be severe         |
| Body Aches       | Mild                           | Severe                        |
| Complications    | Sinusitis, ear infection       | Pneumonia, hospitalisation    |
| Duration         | 7-10 days                      | 1-2 weeks                     |
| Cause            | Rhinovirus (most common)       | Influenza A or B virus        |

TREATMENT:
Common Cold: Rest, fluids, steam inhalation, saline nasal spray, OTC cold medications
             (antihistamines, decongestants), throat lozenges. Antibiotics NOT effective.
Flu: Antiviral medication (Oseltamivir/Tamiflu) — most effective if taken within 48 hours
     Rest, fluids, paracetamol for fever and pain. Flu vaccine (annual) for prevention.

WHEN TO SEE A DOCTOR (Red Flags):
- High fever (above 39.5°C) lasting more than 3 days
- Difficulty breathing or chest pain
- Severe headache or stiff neck
- Confusion or altered consciousness
- Dehydration (not urinating, dry mouth)
- Symptoms that improve then suddenly worsen

SECTION 5: HEART ATTACK — RECOGNIZING WARNING SIGNS
==============================================================

A heart attack (myocardial infarction) occurs when blood flow to part of the heart is blocked,
usually by a blood clot, causing heart muscle damage. It is a medical emergency.

CLASSIC HEART ATTACK WARNING SIGNS:
1. CHEST PAIN or PRESSURE: Feeling of tightness, squeezing, heaviness, or pain in the center
   or left side of the chest, lasting more than a few minutes, or going away and coming back
2. PAIN RADIATING to ARM: Usually the left arm, but can affect both arms, or between shoulder blades
3. SHORTNESS OF BREATH: With or without chest discomfort
4. JAW PAIN: Pain in the jaw, neck, or back
5. NAUSEA, INDIGESTION, HEARTBURN, or ABDOMINAL PAIN (especially in women)
6. COLD SWEATS: Breaking out in a cold sweat without exertion
7. LIGHTHEADEDNESS or SUDDEN DIZZINESS: Feeling faint or dizzy
8. UNUSUAL FATIGUE: Extreme tiredness, especially in women (may occur days before attack)

ATYPICAL SYMPTOMS (More Common in Women, Elderly, Diabetics):
- Sudden extreme fatigue
- Nausea and vomiting
- Back pain
- Jaw pain without chest pain
- Indigestion-like discomfort
- Note: Diabetics may have "silent heart attacks" — no chest pain due to neuropathy!

IF YOU SUSPECT A HEART ATTACK:
1. Call emergency services IMMEDIATELY (ambulance: +91-9876543211 at MedCare)
2. Chew one adult aspirin (325 mg) if not allergic and not contraindicated
3. DO NOT drive yourself to the hospital
4. Loosen tight clothing
5. If the person loses consciousness and stops breathing: start CPR

TIME IS MUSCLE — Every minute of delay damages more heart muscle.
Golden hour: Treatment within 60 minutes of symptom onset gives best outcomes.

SECTION 6: STROKE — FAST RECOGNITION
==============================================================

A stroke occurs when blood supply to part of the brain is cut off (ischemic stroke) or
when a blood vessel in the brain ruptures (hemorrhagic stroke). It is a medical emergency.

FAST — STROKE RECOGNITION GUIDE:
F — FACE DROOPING: Ask the person to smile. Is one side of the face drooping or numb?
A — ARM WEAKNESS: Ask the person to raise both arms. Does one arm drift downward?
S — SPEECH DIFFICULTY: Is speech slurred, strange, or difficult to understand?
    Ask them to repeat a simple phrase: "The sky is blue."
T — TIME TO CALL: If you observe ANY of these signs, call emergency immediately.

BE-FAST (Updated version adds Balance and Eyes):
B — BALANCE: Sudden loss of balance or coordination
E — EYES: Sudden trouble seeing in one or both eyes (double vision, blurred vision)

OTHER STROKE SYMPTOMS:
- Sudden severe headache with no known cause ("worst headache of my life")
- Sudden numbness or weakness of face, arm, or leg — especially on one side
- Sudden confusion or trouble understanding speech
- Sudden difficulty walking, dizziness, or loss of coordination

STROKE FIRST AID:
- Call emergency IMMEDIATELY — do not wait to see if symptoms improve
- Do NOT give food or water (risk of choking with swallowing difficulties)
- Time to treatment is critical — clot-busting drug must be given within 3-4.5 hours
- Note the time symptoms started (critical for treatment decisions)
- Keep person calm, loosen tight clothing, lay person on side if unconscious (recovery position)

SECTION 7: VACCINATION SCHEDULE (INDIA)
==============================================================

ADULT VACCINATION SCHEDULE:

Annually:
- Influenza (Flu) vaccine: Every year for adults, especially elderly, diabetics, heart patients,
  pregnant women, healthcare workers

Every 5 years:
- Pneumococcal vaccine (PCV23): Adults 65+, immunocompromised individuals

Once in lifetime (or boosters):
- Tetanus, Diphtheria, Pertussis (Td/Tdap): Every 10 years (after completing primary series)
- Hepatitis B: 3-dose series if not previously vaccinated
- Hepatitis A: 2-dose series

For specific groups:
- HPV vaccine: Girls and women 9-45 years (2-3 doses depending on age at first dose)
- Meningococcal: College students, pilgrims (Hajj), travelers to endemic areas
- Varicella (Chickenpox): Adults who haven't had chickenpox or vaccine (2 doses)
- Shingles (Zoster): Adults 50+ years

Travel vaccines:
- Typhoid: Every 3 years if at risk, or for travel
- Yellow Fever: Required for travel to endemic Africa and South America
- Japanese Encephalitis: Travelers to endemic areas of Asia
- Cholera: Travelers to high-risk areas
- Rabies: Pre-exposure for high-risk (veterinarians, animal workers)

CHILDREN'S NATIONAL IMMUNIZATION SCHEDULE (NIS — KEY VACCINES):
- Birth: BCG, OPV 0, Hepatitis B 1
- 6 weeks: OPV 1, Pentavalent 1, Rotavirus 1, PCV 1, IPV 1

- 14 weeks: OPV 3, Pentavalent 3, Rotavirus 3, PCV 3, IPV 2
- 9 months: Measles-Rubella (MR) 1, JE vaccine (endemic areas)
- 12 months: Hepatitis A 1, PCV booster
- 15-18 months: DPT booster, OPV booster, MR 2, Varicella 1
- 2 years: Hepatitis A 2, Typhoid
- 5 years: DPT booster

SECTION 8: SLEEP RECOMMENDATIONS
==============================================================

WHY SLEEP MATTERS:
Sleep is essential for physical repair, immune function, memory consolidation, hormone
regulation, and mental health. Chronic poor sleep is linked to obesity, diabetes, heart
disease, depression, and weakened immunity.

RECOMMENDED SLEEP DURATIONS (National Sleep Foundation):
| Age Group             | Recommended Hours           |
|-----------------------|-----------------------------|
| Newborns (0-3 months) | 14–17 hours                 |
| Infants (4-11 months) | 12–15 hours                 |
| Toddlers (1-2 years)  | 11–14 hours                 |
| Preschool (3-5 years) | 10–13 hours                 |
| School age (6-13)     | 9–11 hours                  |
| Teenagers (14-17)     | 8–10 hours                  |
| Adults (18-64)        | 7–9 hours                   |
| Older Adults (65+)    | 7–8 hours                   |

ADULTS NEED 7-9 HOURS OF SLEEP PER NIGHT.

SIGNS OF SLEEP DEPRIVATION:
- Difficulty concentrating or making decisions
- Irritability, mood swings
- Memory problems
- Daytime sleepiness despite nighttime sleep
- Frequent illness (weakened immune system)
- Weight gain (disrupted hunger hormones)

SLEEP HYGIENE TIPS:
- Maintain consistent sleep and wake times — even on weekends
- Keep bedroom cool, dark, and quiet
- Avoid screens (phones, tablets, TV) at least 1 hour before bedtime
- Limit caffeine after 2 PM (coffee, tea, cola, energy drinks)
- Avoid alcohol close to bedtime — it disrupts sleep quality
- Exercise regularly — but not within 2-3 hours of bedtime
- Relaxation techniques: Deep breathing, meditation, progressive muscle relaxation
- Avoid large meals close to bedtime
- A warm bath or shower 1-2 hours before bed can aid sleep

SLEEP DISORDERS:
- Insomnia: Difficulty falling or staying asleep (most common; affects 30% adults)
- Sleep Apnea: Repeated stopping of breathing during sleep; loud snoring; affects 10-30% adults
  Symptoms: Loud snoring, gasping/choking at night, morning headaches, daytime sleepiness
  Treatment: CPAP machine, weight loss, positional therapy, surgery
- Restless Legs Syndrome: Uncomfortable urge to move legs, especially at night
- Narcolepsy: Excessive daytime sleepiness, sudden muscle weakness (cataplexy)

SECTION 9: HYDRATION AND WATER INTAKE
==============================================================

WHY HYDRATION MATTERS:
Water makes up 60% of adult body weight. It is needed for:
- Regulating body temperature (sweating)
- Transporting nutrients and oxygen to cells
- Flushing toxins through urine
- Lubricating joints
- Aiding digestion
- Supporting kidney function

RECOMMENDED WATER INTAKE:
- General guideline: 8 glasses (approximately 2 liters) of water per day
- More accurate: 35 mL per kg of body weight (e.g., 70 kg person = 2.45 liters)
- Men: ~3.7 liters (13 cups) total water per day
- Women: ~2.7 liters (9 cups) total water per day
(Total water includes water from beverages AND food — food accounts for ~20% of intake)

FACTORS REQUIRING MORE WATER:
- Hot weather or high humidity
- Physical exercise or labor
- High fever, vomiting, or diarrhea
- Pregnancy or breastfeeding
- High altitude

SIGNS OF DEHYDRATION:
Mild to Moderate:
- Dry mouth and thirst
- Dark yellow urine (well-hydrated urine is pale yellow)
- Headache
- Fatigue
- Dizziness

Severe (Medical Emergency):
- Very dark urine or no urine
- Extreme thirst
- Confusion or disorientation
- Rapid heartbeat and breathing
- Sunken eyes
- Dry, cool skin

URINE COLOR CHART:
- Pale/clear yellow: Well hydrated
- Dark yellow: Mildly dehydrated, drink more water
- Amber/orange: Dehydrated, drink water urgently
- Brown: Severely dehydrated or possible liver problem — see doctor immediately

TIPS TO DRINK MORE WATER:
- Keep a water bottle on your desk/bag
- Drink a glass of water before each meal
- Set reminders on phone
- Eat water-rich foods: cucumber, watermelon, oranges, tomatoes, lettuce
- Replace sugary drinks with water or unsweetened herbal tea

SECTION 10: MENTAL HEALTH — WHEN TO SEEK HELP
==============================================================

MENTAL HEALTH IS HEALTH:
Mental health includes our emotional, psychological, and social well-being. One in 4 people
will experience a mental health condition in their lifetime. In India, over 150 million people
need mental health care. Seeking help is a sign of strength, not weakness.

COMMON MENTAL HEALTH CONDITIONS:
1. Depression:
   - Persistent sadness, hopelessness, loss of interest in activities for 2+ weeks
   - Changes in sleep and appetite
   - Difficulty concentrating
   - Thoughts of death or suicide
   - Seek help if you experience 5+ symptoms for 2+ weeks

2. Anxiety Disorders:
   - Excessive, uncontrollable worry about everyday events
   - Physical symptoms: Palpitations, sweating, trembling, shortness of breath
   - Panic attacks (sudden intense fear with physical symptoms)
   - Avoidance of feared situations

3. Bipolar Disorder:
   - Alternating episodes of depression (low mood) and mania (elevated, impulsive mood)
   - During mania: Little need for sleep, racing thoughts, risky behavior, grandiosity

4. OCD (Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder):
   - Intrusive, unwanted thoughts (obsessions) + repetitive behaviors to reduce anxiety (compulsions)
   - Handwashing, checking, counting rituals that interfere with daily life

5. PTSD (Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder):
   - Following trauma: flashbacks, nightmares, hypervigilance, emotional numbness
   - Can follow accidents, assault, natural disasters, medical emergencies

WHEN TO SEEK MENTAL HEALTH HELP — URGENTLY:
- Thoughts of suicide or harming yourself: SEEK HELP IMMEDIATELY
  MedCare Emergency: +91-9876543211
  National Suicide Prevention Helpline (India): iCall 9152987821
- Thoughts of harming others
- Psychosis: Hearing or seeing things that aren't there, believing things that aren't true
- Severe depression with inability to function
- Extreme mood swings affecting safety

WHEN TO SEEK MENTAL HEALTH HELP — SOON:
- Persistent sadness, anxiety, or mood changes for more than 2 weeks
- Problems at work, school, or in relationships due to emotional difficulties
- Substance use (alcohol, drugs) to cope with emotions
- Disordered eating habits
- Sleep problems persisting for weeks

HOW TO SUPPORT SOMEONE WITH MENTAL HEALTH ISSUES:
- Listen without judgment
- Ask: "Are you okay?" and really listen to the answer
- Encourage professional help
- Don't minimize their feelings ("Just cheer up!" is NOT helpful)
- Stay connected and check in regularly
- Learn about their condition

SECTION 11: COVID-19 SYMPTOMS AND PRECAUTIONS
==============================================================

COVID-19 SYMPTOMS:
Most Common:
- Fever or chills
- Cough (dry)
- Shortness of breath
- Fatigue

Other Symptoms:
- Loss of taste (ageusia) or smell (anosmia) — hallmark COVID symptoms
- Headache
- Sore throat
- Runny nose or congestion
- Muscle or body aches
- Nausea or vomiting
- Diarrhea

Serious Symptoms (Seek Emergency Care):
- Difficulty breathing or shortness of breath
- Persistent chest pain or pressure
- Confusion or inability to wake
- Bluish lips or face (cyanosis)
- Oxygen saturation below 94%

COVID-19 PRECAUTIONS (Still Relevant):
- Stay up to date with COVID-19 vaccination and boosters
- Wear a mask in crowded or poorly ventilated spaces
- Wash hands frequently with soap and water for at least 20 seconds
- Use hand sanitizer (60%+ alcohol) when soap not available
- Cover coughs and sneezes with elbow
- Maintain distance from sick individuals
- Stay home when you are sick
- Ventilate indoor spaces (open windows)
- Monitor oxygen levels with a pulse oximeter if symptomatic

LONG COVID:
- Symptoms persist for 4+ weeks after acute infection
- Fatigue, brain fog (cognitive difficulties), shortness of breath, joint pain
- Affects ~10-30% of COVID-19 survivors
- Consult a doctor if symptoms persist after recovery

SECTION 12: NUTRITION BASICS
==============================================================

THE SIX ESSENTIAL NUTRIENTS:
1. Carbohydrates: Primary energy source (whole grains, fruits, vegetables, legumes)
2. Proteins: Building blocks of cells (lean meat, fish, eggs, dairy, legumes, nuts)
3. Fats: Energy storage, hormone production, vitamin absorption (healthy fats: olive oil, nuts, avocado)
4. Vitamins: Regulate body processes (found in fruits, vegetables, whole foods)
5. Minerals: Bone health, fluid balance, nerve function (dairy, leafy greens, meat, nuts)
6. Water: Transport, temperature regulation, all metabolic processes

MACRONUTRIENT DISTRIBUTION (ICMR Guidelines for Indians):
- Carbohydrates: 50-60% of total daily calories
- Proteins: 10-15% of total daily calories
- Fats: 20-30% of total daily calories (limit saturated and trans fats)

MICRONUTRIENT DEFICIENCIES COMMON IN INDIA:
- Iron deficiency anemia: Especially in women and children — eat iron-rich foods (spinach, lentils, meat)
- Vitamin D deficiency: Very common — 15 minutes sunlight daily; foods: eggs, fatty fish, fortified milk
- Vitamin B12 deficiency: Especially in vegetarians — eat dairy, eggs; consider supplementation
- Calcium deficiency: Dairy, ragi, sesame seeds, leafy greens
- Iodine deficiency: Use iodized salt

THE HEALTHY PLATE (MyPlate Concept):
- Half the plate: Fruits and vegetables (more vegetables than fruits)
- Quarter of the plate: Lean proteins (legumes, dal, fish, chicken, paneer, eggs)
- Quarter of the plate: Whole grains (whole wheat roti, brown rice, oats)
- Plus: Low-fat dairy (1 cup curd/milk)
- Healthy fat: Small amount (olive oil, nuts, avocado)

FOODS TO MINIMIZE:
- Ultra-processed foods (chips, cookies, instant noodles, packaged snacks)
- Added sugars (soft drinks, sweets, mithai, packaged juices)
- Trans fats (vanaspati, hydrogenated oils in commercial baked goods)
- Excess salt (limit to 5g per day — reduce pickles, papad, processed foods)
- Red and processed meats

SECTION 13: EXERCISE GUIDELINES BY AGE
==============================================================

CHILDREN (6-17 YEARS):
- 60 minutes of moderate-to-vigorous physical activity daily
- Include: Aerobic activity (most of 60 min), muscle-strengthening (3x/week), bone-strengthening (3x/week)
- Examples: Running, cycling, swimming, football, skipping, gymnastics

YOUNG ADULTS (18-39 YEARS):
- 150-300 minutes of moderate-intensity aerobic activity per week
  OR 75-150 minutes of vigorous-intensity per week
- Muscle-strengthening: 2+ days per week (weights, yoga, resistance bands)
- Limit sedentary time

MIDDLE-AGED ADULTS (40-64 YEARS):
- Same aerobic goals as young adults
- Add balance exercises (Tai Chi, yoga, single-leg stands) to prevent falls
- Start strength training if not already doing so
- Flexibility training: Daily stretching

OLDER ADULTS (65+ YEARS):
- 150+ minutes moderate aerobic activity per week (as able)
- Muscle-strengthening: 2+ days per week
- Balance exercises: 3+ days per week (critical for fall prevention)
- Adjust intensity based on health conditions and fitness level
- Even light activity (walking, light housework) is better than nothing
- Consult doctor before starting new exercise program

EXERCISE FOR SPECIFIC CONDITIONS:
- Diabetes: Especially beneficial — 150 min/week; check blood sugar before/after
- Heart disease: Cardiac rehabilitation programs recommended; start slowly
- Arthritis: Low-impact activities (swimming, cycling, water aerobics); avoid high-impact
- Osteoporosis: Weight-bearing exercises (walking, jogging); strength training
- Hypertension: Aerobic exercise most effective; avoid heavy lifting (raises BP acutely)
- Obesity: All types beneficial; start with walking and progress gradually
- Pregnancy: 150 min moderate activity per week; avoid lying on back after 1st trimester; avoid contact sports

SECTION 15: EMERGENCY FIRST AID BASICS
==============================================================

CPR (CARDIOPULMONARY RESUSCITATION):
When to start: Person is unresponsive, not breathing normally
1. Call emergency: +91-9876543211
2. 30 chest compressions: Push hard and fast (5 cm depth) at center of chest, 100-120 per minute
3. 2 rescue breaths: Tilt head back, lift chin, pinch nose, breathe into mouth until chest rises
4. Repeat 30:2 cycle until help arrives or AED is available
5. Hands-only CPR (no rescue breaths) is acceptable if untrained or uncomfortable

CHOKING:
- For conscious adult: 5 back blows (between shoulder blades) followed by 5 abdominal thrusts
  (Heimlich maneuver — upward thrust below sternum)
- If person becomes unconscious: Start CPR
- For infants: 5 back blows + 5 chest thrusts (NOT abdominal thrusts)

SEVERE BLEEDING:
- Apply direct, firm pressure with clean cloth
- Do not remove cloth — add more cloth if soaked through
- Elevate the injured part above heart level
- Apply tourniquet (for limb) only if bleeding is life-threatening and uncontrolled
- Call emergency services

BURNS:
- Cool the burn with cool (not cold/ice) running water for 10-20 minutes
- Do not use ice, butter, toothpaste, or oil on burns
- Do not break blisters
- Cover with clean, non-fluffy material
- Seek medical care for burns larger than palm-size, on face/hands/genitals, or deep burns

FRACTURES:
- Immobilize the injured area — do not try to straighten the bone
- Apply ice pack (wrapped in cloth) to reduce swelling
- Elevate if possible
- Seek emergency care
- For spinal injury: Do not move the person — call emergency

SECTION 16: PREVENTIVE HEALTH SCREENINGS (BY AGE)
==============================================================

AGES 18-39 YEARS:
- Blood pressure: Every 2 years (annually if elevated)
- Blood glucose: Every 3 years if risk factors present
- Cholesterol: Every 5 years (annually if elevated or at risk)
- Dental checkup: Every 6 months
- Eye exam: Every 2 years
- Skin check: Annually (self-examine; doctor if concerns)
- Women: Pap smear every 3 years starting age 21; breast self-exam monthly; mammogram from 40
- Men: Testicular self-exam monthly

AGES 40-64 YEARS:
- Blood pressure: Annually
- Blood glucose (diabetes): Every 3 years; annually if prediabetes
- Cholesterol/Lipid profile: Every 3-5 years
- Colorectal cancer screening: Starting age 45 (colonoscopy or stool tests)
- Mammogram: Annually for women 40-49 (consider); every 1-2 years for women 50+
- Pap smear: Every 3 years or every 5 years with HPV test
- Eye exam: Every 1-2 years
- Thyroid (TSH): Every 5 years (women more prone to thyroid disease)
- Bone density (DEXA): Women at menopause; men at 70

AGES 65+:
- All above screenings
- Bone density: All women; men with risk factors
- Vision and hearing: Annually
- Cognitive assessment: Discuss with doctor
- Fall risk assessment: Annually
- Abdominal aortic aneurysm (AAA) ultrasound: Once for male smokers 65-75 years
