SECTION 1: WHAT IS DIABETES?
============================================================

Diabetes mellitus is a chronic (long-lasting) metabolic disease characterized by elevated levels
of blood glucose (blood sugar), which over time leads to serious damage to the heart, blood vessels,
eyes, kidneys, and nerves. It is one of the most common non-communicable diseases worldwide.

The body's primary source of energy is glucose, which comes from the food we eat. The pancreas —
an organ in the abdomen — produces a hormone called insulin that acts like a key to let blood glucose
enter cells to be used for energy. When the body does not make enough insulin, or cannot effectively
use the insulin it makes, blood sugar levels become too high. This condition is called diabetes.

Key Statistics (Global):
- Over 537 million adults (1 in 10) are living with diabetes worldwide
- In India, over 77 million people are diagnosed with diabetes
- India is called the "Diabetes Capital of the World"
- Diabetes is the leading cause of kidney failure, blindness, and lower limb amputation

SECTION 2: TYPES OF DIABETES
============================================================

2.1 TYPE 1 DIABETES
--------------------
Definition:
Type 1 diabetes is an autoimmune condition in which the body's immune system mistakenly attacks
and destroys the insulin-producing beta cells in the pancreas. As a result, little to no insulin
is produced, and blood glucose levels rise dramatically.

Who Gets Type 1 Diabetes?
- Usually diagnosed in children, teenagers, and young adults (hence previously called juvenile diabetes)
- Can occur at any age
- Affects approximately 5-10% of all diabetes patients
- Not caused by lifestyle or diet choices

Onset:
- Symptoms often come on suddenly and severely
- Can progress to diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) if untreated — a medical emergency

Cause:
- Exact cause unknown
- Believed to involve genetic predisposition + environmental triggers (viral infections)
- NOT caused by eating too much sugar

Treatment:
- Lifelong insulin therapy — cannot be managed with diet alone
- Multiple daily injections or insulin pump (CSII)
- Regular blood glucose monitoring (BGM)
- Continuous glucose monitoring (CGM) sensors

2.2 TYPE 2 DIABETES
--------------------
Definition:
Type 2 diabetes is the most common form of diabetes. The body either does not produce enough
insulin or does not use insulin effectively (insulin resistance). This means glucose builds up
in the blood instead of being used for energy.

Who Gets Type 2 Diabetes?
- Most common in adults, especially over 45 years
- Increasingly seen in younger people, teens, and children due to obesity
- Accounts for approximately 90-95% of all diabetes cases
- Strongly linked to lifestyle factors

Onset:
- Usually develops gradually and slowly over many years
- Many people have prediabetes before full diabetes develops
- Often diagnosed incidentally during routine blood tests

Risk Factors for Type 2 Diabetes:
- Overweight or obesity (BMI > 25)
- Physical inactivity (sedentary lifestyle)
- Family history of diabetes (parent or sibling with diabetes)
- Age above 45 years
- Previous gestational diabetes
- High blood pressure (hypertension) — above 140/90 mmHg
- Abnormal cholesterol levels (high triglycerides, low HDL)
- History of PCOS (Polycystic Ovarian Syndrome)
- Smoking
- Ethnicity: South Asians, African Americans, Hispanics at higher risk

Treatment:
- Lifestyle modifications: diet and exercise
- Oral medications (metformin first-line)
- Injectable medications (GLP-1 agonists, insulin)
- Regular monitoring and follow-ups

2.3 GESTATIONAL DIABETES
-------------------------
Definition:
Gestational diabetes mellitus (GDM) is a type of diabetes that occurs during pregnancy in women
who did not have diabetes before. It develops when the body cannot make enough insulin to meet
the extra needs of pregnancy.

Key Facts:
- Occurs in approximately 10-14% of pregnancies in India
- Usually detected in the second trimester (24-28 weeks)
- Diagnosed by oral glucose tolerance test (OGTT) during pregnancy
- Most cases resolve after delivery, but increases risk of Type 2 diabetes later in life
- The baby is also at increased risk of developing diabetes in the future

Risks of Gestational Diabetes:
For the mother: C-section delivery, pre-eclampsia, future Type 2 diabetes
For the baby: Large birth weight (macrosomia), premature delivery, low blood sugar at birth

Management:
- Blood sugar monitoring throughout pregnancy
- Dietary changes: low glycemic index diet
- Physical activity (safe exercises in pregnancy)
- Insulin injections (if diet and exercise not sufficient)
- No oral medications usually (for safety of baby)

2.4 PREDIABETES
---------------
Definition:
Prediabetes is a condition where blood sugar levels are higher than normal but not high enough
to be classified as Type 2 diabetes. It is a critical warning sign.

Prediabetes Diagnosis Criteria:
- Fasting blood glucose: 100–125 mg/dL (normal is below 100)
- HbA1c: 5.7%–6.4% (normal is below 5.7%)
- 2-hour OGTT glucose: 140–199 mg/dL

Good News About Prediabetes:
- Prediabetes can be reversed with lifestyle changes
- Losing just 5-7% of body weight can reduce risk by 58%
- Regular exercise is highly effective
- Metformin may be prescribed in high-risk individuals

SECTION 3: SYMPTOMS OF DIABETES
============================================================

3.1 GENERAL SYMPTOMS (Common to Type 1 and Type 2)
-----------------------------------------------------
1. Frequent Urination (Polyuria):
   - The kidneys try to filter excess glucose by excreting it in urine
   - May urinate more than 6-7 times per day, including at night (nocturia)

2. Excessive Thirst (Polydipsia):
   - Frequent urination causes dehydration
   - Constant feeling of thirst despite drinking water

3. Unexplained Weight Loss:
   - In Type 1: body breaks down fat and muscle for energy (no insulin)
   - In Type 2: less common but can occur
   - May lose 5-10 kg without trying

4. Increased Hunger (Polyphagia):
   - Cells are not getting glucose (energy), so the brain signals hunger
   - Eating more does not relieve hunger

5. Fatigue and Weakness:
   - Without glucose in cells, body has no fuel
   - Persistent tiredness even after rest

6. Blurred Vision:
   - High blood sugar causes fluid to shift in and out of the eye lens
   - Changes the shape of the lens, leading to blurred vision
   - This can become permanent if untreated (diabetic retinopathy)

7. Slow-Healing Wounds and Cuts:
   - High glucose impairs circulation and immune function
   - Even small cuts may take weeks to heal
   - Dangerous risk of infection and gangrene (especially foot wounds)

8. Frequent Infections:
   - Weakened immune system due to high blood sugar
   - Frequent urinary tract infections (UTIs), skin infections, yeast infections
   - Gum disease (periodontitis) is more common in diabetics

9. Tingling, Numbness, or Burning in Hands and Feet:
   - Caused by diabetic neuropathy (nerve damage from high glucose)
   - Often described as "pins and needles" sensation
   - Usually starts in toes and feet, may progress upward

10. Dark Skin Patches (Acanthosis Nigricans):
    - Dark, velvety patches in skin folds (neck, armpits, groin)
    - A sign of insulin resistance — very common in Type 2 prediabetes

3.2 SYMPTOMS SPECIFIC TO TYPE 1 DIABETES (More Severe and Sudden)
-------------------------------------------------------------------
- Sudden dramatic weight loss
- Nausea and vomiting
- Abdominal pain
- Fruity-smelling breath (due to ketones — sign of DKA)
- Rapid breathing (Kussmaul breathing)
- Altered consciousness or confusion
- These symptoms may appear within days to weeks

3.3 SYMPTOMS SPECIFIC TO TYPE 2 DIABETES (Often Mild and Slow)
---------------------------------------------------------------
- Many people have NO symptoms for years
- Diagnosed during routine blood sugar testing
- Symptoms are often milder and develop over months to years
- Dark skin patches (acanthosis nigricans) are common early signs

3.4 WHEN TO SEEK IMMEDIATE EMERGENCY CARE
------------------------------------------
Go to emergency immediately if you experience:
- Extremely high blood sugar (>500 mg/dL) with confusion or loss of consciousness
- Fruity-smelling breath with rapid breathing (Diabetic Ketoacidosis — DKA)
- Severe low blood sugar (hypoglycemia) with loss of consciousness
- High-grade fever with infection in a diabetic foot wound
- Chest pain (heart attack risk is higher in diabetics)

SECTION 4: DIAGNOSIS OF DIABETES
============================================================

4.1 FASTING BLOOD GLUCOSE TEST
- Patient fasts for at least 8 hours (no food or drink except water)
- Blood sample taken in the morning
- Interpretation:
  * Normal: Less than 100 mg/dL (5.6 mmol/L)
  * Prediabetes: 100–125 mg/dL (5.6–6.9 mmol/L)
  * Diabetes: 126 mg/dL or higher (7.0 mmol/L or higher) on two separate occasions

4.2 HbA1c (GLYCATED HEMOGLOBIN) TEST
- Measures average blood sugar over the past 2-3 months
- No fasting required — can be done any time of day
- Reflects how well blood sugar has been controlled over time
- Interpretation:
  * Normal: Below 5.7%
  * Prediabetes: 5.7%–6.4%
  * Diabetes: 6.5% or higher
  * Target for diabetics: Below 7.0% (ideally below 6.5% for younger, healthy individuals)
  * Acceptable for elderly or high-risk: Below 8.0%

4.3 RANDOM (CASUAL) BLOOD GLUCOSE TEST
- Blood sample taken any time, regardless of last meal
- Interpretation:
  * Normal: Below 140 mg/dL
  * Prediabetes range: 140–199 mg/dL
  * Diabetes: 200 mg/dL or higher WITH diabetes symptoms

4.4 ORAL GLUCOSE TOLERANCE TEST (OGTT)
- Patient fasts for 8 hours
- Drinks 75g of glucose solution
- Blood glucose measured after 2 hours
- Interpretation (2-hour glucose):
  * Normal: Below 140 mg/dL
  * Prediabetes: 140–199 mg/dL
  * Diabetes: 200 mg/dL or higher
- Primarily used for gestational diabetes screening (100g glucose for pregnant women)

4.5 ADDITIONAL TESTS OFTEN ORDERED FOR DIABETICS
- Fasting insulin level (to assess insulin resistance)
- C-peptide (to determine insulin production capacity)
- Kidney function (creatinine, BUN, eGFR)
- Urine microalbumin (early kidney damage detection)
- Lipid profile (cholesterol levels)
- Liver function tests
- Thyroid function tests (thyroid disease is common in diabetics)
- Complete blood count (CBC)
- Eye examination (dilated fundus exam for retinopathy)
- Foot examination (peripheral neuropathy assessment)

SECTION 5: TREATMENT OF DIABETES
============================================================

5.1 LIFESTYLE MANAGEMENT (CORNERSTONE OF TREATMENT)

Lifestyle changes are the first and most important step in managing Type 2 diabetes and
preventing its complications. They are also beneficial for Type 1 patients.

Diet:
- Reduce intake of refined carbohydrates (white rice, white bread, sugar)
- Increase dietary fiber (vegetables, whole grains, legumes)
- Eat low glycemic index (GI) foods
- Control portion sizes
- Distribute meals evenly throughout the day (3 main meals + 2 small snacks)
- Limit alcohol consumption
- Completely avoid sugary drinks (soda, fruit juice, energy drinks)

Exercise:
- Aim for 150 minutes per week of moderate-intensity aerobic exercise
  (brisk walking, swimming, cycling)
- Strength/resistance training 2-3 times per week (weights, resistance bands)
- Even short walks after meals significantly reduce post-meal blood sugar spikes
- Avoid prolonged sitting — stand or walk every 30 minutes
- Exercise improves insulin sensitivity and helps with weight loss

Weight Management:
- Losing 5-10% of body weight significantly improves blood sugar control
- Even 5-7 kg weight loss can reduce HbA1c by 1-2%
- Calorie deficit of 500 kcal/day leads to ~0.5 kg/week weight loss

Stress Management:
- Stress hormones (cortisol, adrenaline) raise blood glucose
- Practice yoga, meditation, deep breathing
- Adequate sleep (7-8 hours per night)
- Seek mental health support if needed (depression is twice as common in diabetics)

Smoking Cessation:
- Smoking significantly worsens cardiovascular risk in diabetics
- Increases risk of kidney disease and nerve damage
- Seek help: nicotine replacement or medications available

5.2 ORAL MEDICATIONS FOR DIABETES

First-Line Medication: METFORMIN
- Most commonly prescribed diabetes medication globally
- Reduces glucose production by the liver
- Improves insulin sensitivity
- Does not cause weight gain or hypoglycemia
- Very affordable and widely available
- Dosage: Usually 500 mg twice daily with meals, titrated up
- Side effects: Nausea, diarrhea (usually temporary), rare vitamin B12 deficiency
- Contraindicated in: Kidney failure (eGFR < 30), liver disease, contrast dye procedures

Second-Line Medications:
1. Sulfonylureas (Glipizide, Glyburide, Glimepiride):
   - Stimulate pancreas to produce more insulin
   - Risk of hypoglycemia (low blood sugar)
   - May cause weight gain

2. DPP-4 Inhibitors (Sitagliptin, Vildagliptin, Alogliptin):
   - Increase insulin release after meals
   - Weight-neutral
   - Low risk of hypoglycemia

3. SGLT-2 Inhibitors (Empagliflozin, Dapagliflozin, Canagliflozin):
   - Cause kidneys to excrete excess glucose in urine
   - Cause weight loss
   - Beneficial for heart and kidney protection
   - Risk of urinary tract infections, genital fungal infections

4. GLP-1 Receptor Agonists (Liraglutide, Semaglutide — injectable):
   - Increase insulin, decrease glucagon
   - Promote weight loss
   - Excellent cardiovascular protection
   - May cause nausea initially

5. Thiazolidinediones (Pioglitazone):
   - Improve insulin resistance
   - May cause fluid retention and weight gain

5.3 INSULIN THERAPY

Types of Insulin:
1. Rapid-acting (Aspart, Lispro, Glulisine):
   - Taken just before meals
   - Peaks in 1-2 hours

2. Short-acting (Regular insulin):
   - Taken 30 minutes before meals

3. Intermediate-acting (NPH):
   - Covers glucose for about 12 hours

4. Long-acting (Glargine/Lantus, Detemir, Degludec):
   - Once or twice daily injection
   - Provides steady, 24-hour background insulin coverage

5. Pre-mixed insulins:
   - Combinations of rapid + intermediate acting
   - Used twice daily

Insulin Delivery Methods:
- Syringe and needle (most economical)
- Insulin pen (convenient, accurate dosing)
- Insulin pump (continuous subcutaneous insulin infusion — most advanced)
- Insulin jet injector (needle-free)

Who Needs Insulin?
- All Type 1 diabetics: Lifelong insulin required
- Type 2 diabetics when: HbA1c remains high despite maximum oral medication,
  during illness/surgery/pregnancy, or when pancreas significantly exhausted

5.4 BLOOD GLUCOSE MONITORING

Self-Monitoring of Blood Glucose (SMBG):
- Use a glucometer and test strips
- For Type 1: Test 4-8 times daily (before meals, 2 hours after meals, bedtime, overnight)
- For Type 2 on insulin: 2-4 times daily
- For Type 2 on oral medications: 1-2 times daily or as advised
- Log all readings in a diary or app for doctor review

Continuous Glucose Monitoring (CGM):
- Small sensor worn on skin (usually upper arm or abdomen)
- Provides real-time glucose readings every 5 minutes
- Alerts for high and low glucose levels
- Examples: FreeStyle Libre (most popular in India), Dexcom, Medtronic

Target Blood Glucose Levels:
- Fasting (before meals): 80–130 mg/dL
- 2 hours after meals: Less than 180 mg/dL
- Bedtime: 100–140 mg/dL
- HbA1c target: Less than 7% (individualized by doctor)

SECTION 6: DIABETIC DIET AND NUTRITION
============================================================

6.1 PRINCIPLES OF DIABETIC DIET
- No single "diabetic diet" — it is a healthy, balanced approach
- Focus on what you eat, how much you eat, and when you eat
- Consistent meal timing helps stabilize blood sugar
- Work with a registered dietitian for a personalized plan

6.2 FOODS TO CHOOSE (LOW GLYCEMIC INDEX FOODS)
Vegetables (non-starchy): Spinach, broccoli, cauliflower, cabbage, bitter gourd (karela),
  cucumber, tomato, beans, methi (fenugreek)
Fruits (in moderation): Guava, papaya, apple, pear, berries, citrus fruits
Whole grains: Brown rice, whole wheat bread, oats, millet (bajra, jowar, ragi), barley
Legumes: Lentils (dal), chickpeas, kidney beans, moong beans
Lean proteins: Fish, chicken (grilled), eggs, low-fat dairy (paneer, curd/yogurt)
Healthy fats: Olive oil, nuts (almonds, walnuts), seeds (flaxseed, chia seeds), avocado

6.3 FOODS TO AVOID OR LIMIT
- Sugar: White sugar, jaggery, honey, glucose (all spike blood sugar rapidly)
- Sugary drinks: Soft drinks, fruit juices, milkshakes, energy drinks
- Refined carbohydrates: White bread, maida (white flour) products, processed snacks
- Fried and fatty foods: Samosas, pakoras, chips, fried rice
- White rice: Eat small portions; better: switch to brown rice or millets
- Full-fat dairy: Whole milk, cream, butter in excess
- Alcohol: Increases hypoglycemia risk, high in empty calories
- Processed meats: Sausages, bacon, ham (increase cardiovascular risk)

6.4 SAMPLE DIABETIC MEAL PLAN (INDIAN CONTEXT)

EARLY MORNING (6-7 AM):
- 1 glass warm water with methi seeds (soaked overnight)
- Or 1 glass warm water with lemon (no sugar)

BREAKFAST (8-9 AM):
- 2 whole wheat rotis with vegetable sabzi (no potato)
- Or oatmeal with nuts and seeds (no sugar)
- Or 2 egg whites scrambled with vegetables
- 1 cup low-fat milk or curd (no sugar)
- Avoid: Poha, upma (high GI unless modified), parathas with butter

MID-MORNING SNACK (11 AM):
- Small handful of mixed nuts (almonds, walnuts)
- Or 1 small fruit (guava, apple, pear)

LUNCH (1-2 PM):
- 2 whole wheat rotis + 1 small bowl brown rice
- Dal (any lentil — excellent protein and fiber)
- 2 vegetable dishes (include green leafy vegetables)
- Curd (yogurt) — 1 small bowl
- Salad (cucumber, tomato, onion with lemon)
- Avoid: White rice in large quantity, fried items, pickles in excess

EVENING SNACK (4-5 PM):
- Roasted chana (chickpeas) — small handful
- Or sprouts chaat (without sweet chutney)
- 1 cup green tea or unsweetened herbal tea

DINNER (7-8 PM):
- 2 whole wheat rotis
- 1 bowl dal or lean protein (fish, chicken, paneer)
- 1-2 vegetable dishes
- Salad
- Avoid eating after 9 PM

BEDTIME SNACK (if blood sugar tends to drop overnight):
- 1 small glass milk or handful nuts

6.5 GLYCEMIC INDEX (GI) EXPLAINED
- Low GI (below 55): Oats, lentils, most vegetables, apples, pears, milk
- Medium GI (56-69): Brown rice, whole wheat bread, banana, mango
- High GI (70+): White bread, white rice, glucose, corn flakes, watermelon, potato

Lower GI foods cause a slower, smaller rise in blood sugar — better for diabetics.

SECTION 7: EXERCISE AND DIABETES
============================================================

7.1 BENEFITS OF EXERCISE FOR DIABETICS
- Lowers blood glucose levels directly (muscles use glucose during exercise)
- Improves insulin sensitivity (cells respond better to insulin)
- Promotes weight loss and maintenance
- Reduces cardiovascular risk (lowers blood pressure, cholesterol)
- Improves mood and mental health
- Reduces HbA1c by 0.5-1.5%

7.2 RECOMMENDED EXERCISE (ADA GUIDELINES)
- 150 minutes/week of moderate-intensity aerobic exercise
  Examples: Brisk walking, cycling, swimming, dancing, Zumba
- OR 75 minutes/week of vigorous-intensity exercise
  Examples: Running, HIIT, singles tennis
- Strength training: 2-3 times per week
  Examples: Resistance bands, weight machines, bodyweight exercises (squats, push-ups)
- Flexibility: Yoga and stretching 2-3 times per week (especially beneficial for Indians)
- IMPORTANT: No more than 2 consecutive days without aerobic exercise

7.3 EXERCISE SAFETY TIPS FOR DIABETICS
- Check blood sugar BEFORE exercise:
  * Below 100 mg/dL: Eat a small snack first (15g carbs)
  * 100-250 mg/dL: Safe to exercise
  * Above 250 mg/dL with ketones: Do NOT exercise — call your doctor
- Carry fast-acting glucose (glucose tablets, juice) during exercise
- Stay hydrated — drink water before, during, and after exercise
- Wear proper footwear (never exercise barefoot — neuropathy causes undetected injuries)
- Exercise with a partner if possible
- Inform workout partner of your diabetic status and emergency procedure
- Monitor blood sugar after exercise (can drop for hours post-exercise)

SECTION 8: COMPLICATIONS OF DIABETES
============================================================

8.1 LONG-TERM COMPLICATIONS (CHRONIC)

DIABETIC NEUROPATHY (Nerve Damage):
- Most common complication — affects 50% of diabetics
- Peripheral neuropathy: Tingling, numbness, burning in feet and hands
- Autonomic neuropathy: Affects heart rate, blood pressure, digestion, bladder
- Diabetic foot: Neuropathy + poor circulation = dangerous wounds
- Prevention: Tight blood sugar control, regular foot examinations

DIABETIC RETINOPATHY (Eye Damage):
- Leading cause of blindness in working-age adults globally
- Damages blood vessels in the retina
- Early stages: No symptoms (annual eye exam critical)
- Advanced stages: Blurred vision, floaters, vision loss
- Treatment: Laser therapy, anti-VEGF injections, surgery
- Prevention: Control blood sugar and blood pressure

DIABETIC NEPHROPATHY (Kidney Damage):
- Chronic kidney disease due to damaged kidney blood vessels
- Early sign: Microalbuminuria (protein in urine)
- Progresses to chronic kidney disease, may require dialysis or transplant
- Prevention: Control blood sugar, blood pressure, use of SGLT-2 inhibitors or ACE inhibitors

CARDIOVASCULAR DISEASE:
- Diabetics have 2-4x higher risk of heart attack and stroke
- Accelerated atherosclerosis (artery hardening)
- Silent heart attacks more common in diabetics (no chest pain due to neuropathy)
- Management: Statins, blood pressure control, aspirin (as prescribed)

DIABETIC FOOT DISEASE:
- Combination of neuropathy and peripheral vascular disease
- Small wounds can progress to serious infections, gangrene, amputation
- Daily foot inspection critical
- Foot care: Wash and dry feet daily, moisturize, trim nails carefully, wear proper footwear
- Never walk barefoot — even at home

8.2 ACUTE COMPLICATIONS (EMERGENCIES)

HYPOGLYCEMIA (Low Blood Sugar):
- Blood glucose below 70 mg/dL
- Symptoms: Shaking/trembling, cold sweats, palpitations, anxiety, hunger, pale skin,
  confusion, dizziness, headache, weakness
- Severe: Seizures, unconsciousness
- Causes: Too much insulin, skipped meals, excessive exercise, alcohol
- Treatment (Rule of 15):
  * Eat/drink 15g fast-acting carbs (3-4 glucose tablets, 1/2 cup orange juice,
    1 tablespoon sugar dissolved in water)
  * Wait 15 minutes, recheck blood sugar
  * If still below 70, repeat
  * Eat a proper meal/snack once glucose normalizes
  * For unconscious patient: Glucagon injection (emergency kit), call ambulance immediately

HYPERGLYCEMIA (High Blood Sugar):
- Blood glucose significantly above target (usually above 250 mg/dL)
- Symptoms: Extreme thirst, frequent urination, headache, fatigue, blurred vision
- Causes: Missed medication, eating too much, illness, stress
- Management: Extra hydration (water), check for illness, contact doctor

DIABETIC KETOACIDOSIS (DKA) — EMERGENCY:
- Primarily in Type 1 diabetes, rare in Type 2
- Body breaks down fat for fuel, producing ketones (acidic)
- Symptoms: Nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, fruity breath, rapid breathing, confusion
- Life-threatening — requires immediate hospitalization
- Treatment: IV fluids, insulin drip, electrolyte correction

HYPEROSMOLAR HYPERGLYCEMIC STATE (HHS) — EMERGENCY:
- Primarily in elderly Type 2 diabetics
- Extremely high blood sugar (>600 mg/dL) without significant ketones
- Severe dehydration, confusion, seizures, coma
- Requires immediate hospitalization

SECTION 9: PREVENTION OF DIABETES
============================================================

9.1 CAN TYPE 2 DIABETES BE PREVENTED?
YES! Type 2 diabetes is largely preventable with lifestyle changes.
The landmark Diabetes Prevention Program (DPP) study proved:
- Lifestyle changes (diet + exercise + weight loss) reduced diabetes risk by 58%
- Metformin reduced risk by 31%
- These effects lasted 10+ years

9.2 STRATEGIES FOR PREVENTION

Achieve and Maintain Healthy Weight:
- Every 1 kg of weight loss reduces diabetes risk
- Target BMI: 18.5-24.9 (Asian Indians should aim for BMI < 23)
- Waist circumference target: Men < 90 cm, Women < 80 cm (Indian guidelines)

Be Physically Active:
- At least 30 minutes of moderate exercise daily, 5 days a week
- Even light activity (gardening, walking while talking on phone) helps
- Reduce sitting time — take breaks every 30-60 minutes

Eat Healthy:
- Follow a low glycemic index diet
- Increase vegetables, fruits (not juice), whole grains, legumes
- Reduce saturated fats, trans fats, refined carbohydrates, added sugars

Stop Smoking:
- Smoking increases diabetes risk by 30-40%
- Seek help to quit — medications and patches available

Regular Health Screenings:
- Fasting blood glucose every year if you have risk factors
- HbA1c every 1-2 years for those over 40 or with risk factors
- Blood pressure, cholesterol checks annually

9.3 WHO SHOULD BE SCREENED FOR DIABETES?
- All adults aged 45 and older (every 3 years if normal)
- Adults of any age who are overweight/obese AND have one risk factor
- Women who had gestational diabetes
- People with prediabetes (annually)
- People with first-degree relatives with diabetes
- High-risk ethnic groups: South Asians, African Americans, Hispanics

SECTION 10: LIVING WITH DIABETES
============================================================

10.1 MONITORING AND REGULAR FOLLOW-UPS
- Fasting blood glucose: Daily (if on insulin) or weekly/monthly (if on oral medications)
- HbA1c: Every 3 months until target achieved, then every 6 months
- Kidney function (creatinine, urine albumin): Annually
- Lipid profile: Annually
- Liver function: Annually (especially if on medications)
- Eye examination: Annually (dilated fundus exam by ophthalmologist)
- Foot examination: At every clinic visit
- Blood pressure: At every visit
- Dental checkup: Every 6 months (gum disease is more common in diabetics)
- Thyroid function: Every 1-2 years

10.2 MEDICATION ADHERENCE
- Never stop medications without doctor's advice
- Even if blood sugar appears normal — the medication is working!
- Set reminders on phone for medication times
- Pill organizers help track daily medications
- Carry emergency medication information card in wallet
- Inform all healthcare providers you are diabetic and list your medications

10.3 MENTAL HEALTH AND DIABETES
- "Diabetes distress" affects 20-40% of people with diabetes
- Depression is twice as common in diabetics
- Signs of diabetes distress: Feeling overwhelmed, angry, burned out about diabetes management
- Solutions: Peer support groups, counseling, mental health professional help
- MedCare offers psychiatric support for diabetic patients — contact Dr. Arjun Mehta

10.4 DIABETIC FOOT CARE (VERY IMPORTANT)
Daily routine:
1. Inspect feet every day (use mirror for sole if vision is limited)
2. Wash feet with lukewarm water and mild soap, dry gently between toes
3. Apply moisturizer (not between toes)
4. Trim toenails straight across, file rough edges
5. Never cut corns or calluses yourself
6. Always wear well-fitting shoes and cotton socks
7. Never walk barefoot — even indoors
8. Check inside shoes before wearing for foreign objects

See doctor immediately if you notice:
- Any wound, blister, or ulcer on the foot
- Redness, warmth, or swelling
- Discharge or bad smell from foot wound
- Color change in toes or foot (blue, black, white)

SECTION 11: FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS ABOUT DIABETES
============================================================

Q: Can diabetes be cured?
A: Type 1 diabetes currently has no cure. Type 2 diabetes can go into remission (blood
   sugar normalizes without medication) with significant weight loss and lifestyle changes,
   but careful monitoring continues for life.

Q: Is diabetes genetic? Will my children get it?
A: Type 1 diabetes has a genetic component — if one parent has Type 1, a child has about
   5-10% risk. Type 2 has a stronger genetic link — if a parent has Type 2, the child has
   about 40% lifetime risk, but this can be dramatically reduced by lifestyle choices.

Q: Can I eat fruit with diabetes?
A: Yes! Fruits are healthy and should be part of a diabetic diet in moderation. Choose
   low-GI fruits (guava, apple, pear, citrus). Limit high-sugar fruits (mango, banana, grapes)
   to small portions. Avoid fruit juices — always eat whole fruit.

Q: Is it safe to exercise when blood sugar is high?
A: If blood sugar is below 250 mg/dL and you feel well, mild to moderate exercise is safe.
   If blood sugar is above 250 mg/dL AND you have ketones in urine, do NOT exercise — rest
   and contact your doctor.

Q: How do I know if I have hypoglycemia?
A: Common signs: Shaking, sweating, palpitations, dizziness, hunger, pale skin, confusion.
   Treat immediately with 15g fast-acting carbs (glucose tablets, juice, sugar solution).

Q: What is a normal blood sugar level?
A: Fasting: 70-99 mg/dL; After meals (2 hours): Less than 140 mg/dL; HbA1c: Below 5.7%.
   For diagnosed diabetics, targets are individualized by doctor.

Q: Does eating too much sugar cause diabetes?
A: Not directly. However, consuming excess sugar (and calories) leads to weight gain and
   obesity — major risk factors for Type 2 diabetes. Type 1 is not caused by diet at all.
